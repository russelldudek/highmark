Central operating contradiction

Highmark must move quickly enough to create meaningful operational and affordability gains while preserving qualified human decisions, privacy, regulatory compliance, member trust, provider trust, and reliable implementation.

Five tensions to manage

Opportunity breadth vs. capacity

Many workflows can plausibly benefit; only a bounded portfolio can receive the evidence, integration, adoption, and leadership attention required.

Proof speed vs. responsible governance

Legal, privacy, business, technical, and human-authority conditions must shape the proof rather than arrive as late-stage approval gates.

Technical feasibility vs. frontline adoption

A model can work while the operating intervention fails. Workflow fit, training, exception design, and ownership determine whether value survives contact with work.

Local improvement vs. enterprise reuse

HPO needs to learn which components, controls, evidence patterns, and change interventions should compound across workflows.

Efficiency pressure vs. trust

Administrative efficiency matters, but the method must protect the experience and authority of members, providers, clinicians, and employees.

Decision standard

Advance only when the problem, evidence, authority, adoption owner, measurement, and scale/stop criteria can be stated in one coherent opportunity charter.

Executive discussion guide

Questions before prescriptions

Discover the operating reality, decision rights, and change burden.

Mandate and portfolio

  1. Where is HPO experiencing the greatest gap between AI interest and implementation capacity?
  2. How are opportunities currently groomed, prioritized, paused, and stopped?
  3. Which existing use cases have executive sponsorship but still struggle with workflow ownership or adoption?

Authority, governance, and evidence

  1. At what point do Responsible AI, privacy, legal, security, and data-stewardship partners enter the opportunity process?
  2. Which decisions create the most debate about human authority, source provenance, or acceptable risk?
  3. What evidence must exist for a proof to move into scaled use?

Adoption and value

  1. Who owns value realization after a proof moves from the AI team into an operating function?
  2. How does HPO measure adoption beyond access or usage?
  3. Which workflow frictions repeat across member, provider, claims, utilization, network, appeals, and care-management teams?

First-six-month calibration

  1. What would make the first six months unmistakably valuable to the Director of AI and HPO leaders?
  2. Which capability should this person strengthen immediately: discovery, proof discipline, implementation, adoption, portfolio management, or value capture?
  3. What is the strongest concern about my fit that a 15-minute working discussion could test directly?

What I would listen for

Decision frictionWhere opportunities wait, repeat, escalate, or consume scarce technical and leadership attention.
Ownership ambiguityWhere sponsorship exists but changed work, exceptions, training, or value realization lack an accountable owner.
Evidence discontinuityWhere current-state baselines, source authority, user feedback, or post-deployment learning are not connected.
